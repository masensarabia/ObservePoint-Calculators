Key Terms: Violation and Cure Period

What is a Violation?

In the context of data privacy laws, a violation refers to any action or inaction that breaches the specific requirements set by the law. Violations can vary widely depending on the regulation, but they typically include actions like unauthorized data collection, failure to secure personal information, not honoring opt-out requests, or mishandling sensitive data. Each occurrence of a breach (e.g., each instance of failing to delete user data upon request) is considered a separate violation, and fines are often calculated on a "per violation" basis.

For example, suppose a company tracks 10,000 unique visitors in the month of June using unauthorized technologies (like cookies or pixels) without obtaining user consent. This could result in 10,000 individual violations, as each unauthorized tracking instance is treated as a separate breach.

Source: https://secureprivacy.ai/blog/understanding-cookie-compliance

What is a Cure Period?

A cure period is the time frame given to a company to correct or "cure" a violation once it has been identified. During this period, the company can address the issue without facing penalties. However, not all states or regulations offer a cure period, and the length of the cure period can vary. If a company fails to remedy the violation within the specified cure period, it may then be subject to fines and penalties.

For instance, under the Colorado Privacy Act (CPA), companies have 60 days to cure a violation before fines are imposed, but this provision expires on January 1, 2025. After that date, violations may result in immediate penalties.

Key Takeaway: Understanding these terms is crucial for grasping how data privacy regulations operate and how non-compliance can lead to significant financial consequences.

Introduction to Data Privacy Violations

Why Compliance Matters

Data privacy laws are designed to protect consumers' personal information. Non-compliance with these laws can lead to significant financial penalties, damage to reputation, and loss of customer trust. Understanding the different ways in which your company could violate these laws is essential for mitigating risks.

Common Ways to Violate Data Privacy Laws

Tracking Data Without Consent

Deploying cookies or tracking mechanisms without user opt-in consent is a violation in many jurisdictions:

  • California (CCPA/CPRA): Requires explicit opt-in consent, especially for minors.
  • Colorado Privacy Act (CPA): Explicit consent is required for sensitive data.
  • Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (VCDPA): Requires consent before processing sensitive data.
  • GDPR: Consent is also required for most types of data processing, particularly sensitive data.

Sources: CCPA Overview, State of California Legislature, CPA Overview, State of Colorado, GDPR Consent Requirements, European Commission

Not Honoring Opt-Out Requests

Failure to comply with opt-out requests can lead to hefty fines:

  • California (CCPA): Must stop processing/selling personal information when users opt out.
  • Virginia (VCDPA): Similar provisions requiring businesses to honor opt-out requests promptly.

Sources: California CCPA Compliance, Attorney General's Office, VCDPA Law Overview

Inadequate Security Measures Leading to Data Breach

Not implementing strong security measures leads to violations in many jurisdictions:

  • New York (SHIELD Act): Requires reasonable safeguards to protect personal data.
  • Texas (Texas Identity Theft Enforcement and Protection Act): Requires businesses to implement data security measures.
  • GDPR: Violations of Article 32 (Security of Processing) if there is a breach due to weak safeguards.

Sources: SHIELD Act Overview, GDPR Security Requirements, European Commission

Processing Sensitive Data Without Proper Safeguards

Processing health or biometric data without safeguards violates multiple laws:

  • California (CCPA): Specific consent is required for processing sensitive data.
  • Colorado (CPA): Consent is required before processing sensitive data like health, race, or biometric information.
  • GDPR: Processing sensitive personal data without explicit consent or safeguards can lead to violations.

Sources: CCPA on Sensitive Data, California Attorney General, Colorado Privacy Act Overview, GDPR Article 9, European Commission

Failing to Provide Access to Data

Failing to provide consumers access to their personal data when requested:

  • California (CCPA): Consumers can request access to their data, and businesses must comply within 45 days.
  • Colorado (CPA): Businesses must respond to access requests in a timely manner.
  • GDPR: Article 15 allows individuals to request access to their data.

Sources: CCPA Access Rights Overview, Colorado Privacy Act Rights, GDPR Data Access Rights, European Commission

Ignoring Data Deletion Requests (Right to be Forgotten)

Failing to delete consumer data when requested is a violation in many regions:

  • California (CCPA): Right to delete personal information is enshrined in the law.
  • Virginia (VCDPA): Similar provisions for the right to deletion of personal information.
  • GDPR: Article 17 covers the right to be forgotten.

Sources: CCPA Right to Delete Information, GDPR Right to be Forgotten

Failure to Comply with Data Subject Rights

This includes violations for not allowing consumers to exercise their rights:

  • Right to Correct Data: Consumers can request corrections to inaccurate data.
  • Right to Data Portability: Consumers can request data in a portable, transferable format.

Sources: CCPA Right to Correct Information, GDPR Article 16 (Data Rectification), GDPR Article 20 (Data Portability)

Processing Data Without a Legal Basis

Processing personal data without one of the legal bases outlined in privacy laws:

  • Virginia (VCDPA): Personal data cannot be processed without consent, a contract, or legitimate interest.
  • GDPR: Article 6 outlines lawful bases for processing data, including consent and legitimate interest.

Sources: VCDPA Overview, GDPR Article 6 (Legal Bases)

Failure to Notify of a Data Breach in Time

Failing to notify individuals or authorities of a data breach within the mandated time frame:

  • California (CCPA/CPRA): Requires timely notification of affected consumers if a data breach occurs.
  • GDPR: Article 33 mandates notification of a data breach within 72 hours.

Sources: CCPA Breach Notification Requirements, GDPR Article 33 (Breach Notification)

Failure to Limit Data Collection to What’s Necessary (Data Minimization)

Collecting more data than needed for the specified purpose:

  • California (CCPA/CPRA): Limits on data collection to what is reasonably necessary.
  • GDPR: Article 5(1)(c) covers the principle of data minimization.

Sources: CCPA Data Minimization Requirements, GDPR Article 5 (Data Minimization)

Improper Handling of Children’s Data

Collecting or processing children’s data without proper parental consent or safeguards:

  • California (CCPA/CPRA): Special protections for children under 16.
  • COPPA (U.S.): Strict parental consent requirements for children under 13.
  • GDPR: Article 8 outlines requirements for parental consent for children under 16.

Sources: CCPA Children’s Data Protections, COPPA, Federal Trade Commission, GDPR Article 8 (Children’s Data)

GDPR Violations and Fines

Tracking Data Without Consent

Under GDPR, tracking users without their explicit consent—such as through cookies, analytics pixels, or other tracking mechanisms—is a direct violation. The GDPR mandates that consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous.

Source: EUR-Lex, Official Text of GDPR Regulation 2016/679

Not Honoring Data Subject Requests

GDPR grants several rights to data subjects, including the right to access their data, request corrections, and request deletion (the "right to be forgotten"). Failure to comply with these requests within one month constitutes a violation.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Articles 15-18

Data Breaches Due to Inadequate Security Measures

GDPR requires organizations to implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk. A data breach due to insufficient security measures is a violation.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Article 32

Improper Data Processing Beyond Original Purpose

Data must be processed for the specific purpose initially communicated to the data subject. Processing the data for any new purpose without additional consent is a violation.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Article 6(4)

Non-compliance with Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)

Organizations must conduct DPIAs when processing activities pose a high risk to individuals' rights and freedoms. Failing to do so can lead to significant fines under GDPR.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Article 35

GDPR Fines

GDPR fines are categorized in two tiers:

  • Lower Tier: Up to €10 million, or 2% of the worldwide annual revenue of the prior financial year, whichever is higher.
  • Higher Tier: Up to €20 million, or 4% of the worldwide annual revenue of the prior financial year, whichever is higher.

Source: European Commission, GDPR Overview and Text
