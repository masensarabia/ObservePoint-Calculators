Key Terms: Violation and Cure Period

What is a Violation?

In the context of data privacy laws, a violation refers to any action or inaction that breaches the specific requirements set by the law. Violations can vary widely depending on the regulation, but they typically include actions like unauthorized data collection, failure to secure personal information, not honoring opt-out requests, or mishandling sensitive data. Each occurrence of a breach (e.g., each instance of failing to delete user data upon request) is considered a separate violation, and fines are often calculated on a "per violation" basis.

For example, suppose a company tracks 10,000 unique visitors in the month of June using unauthorized technologies (like cookies or pixels) without obtaining user consent. This could result in 10,000 individual violations, as each unauthorized tracking instance is treated as a separate breach.

Source: https://secureprivacy.ai/blog/understanding-cookie-compliance

What is a Cure Period?

A cure period is the time frame given to a company to correct or "cure" a violation once it has been identified. During this period, the company can address the issue without facing penalties. However, not all states or regulations offer a cure period, and the length of the cure period can vary. If a company fails to remedy the violation within the specified cure period, it may then be subject to fines and penalties.

For instance, under the Colorado Privacy Act (CPA), companies have 60 days to cure a violation before fines are imposed, but this provision expires on January 1, 2025. After that date, violations may result in immediate penalties.

Key Takeaway: Understanding these terms is crucial for grasping how data privacy regulations operate and how non-compliance can lead to significant financial consequences.

Introduction to Data Privacy Violations

Why Compliance Matters

Data privacy laws are designed to protect consumers' personal information. Non-compliance with these laws can lead to significant financial penalties, damage to reputation, and loss of customer trust. Understanding the different ways in which your company could violate these laws is essential for mitigating risks.

Common Ways to Violate Data Privacy Laws

Tracking Data Without Consent

Deploying an analytics pixel or cookie on a website that tracks users without obtaining their explicit opt-in consent. This is required under laws like the GDPR and CCPA.

Source: California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), State of California Legislature

Not Honoring Opt-Out Requests

Continuing to process or sell a consumer’s personal data after they have opted out. This is required under laws like the Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (VCDPA).

Source: Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (VCDPA), Commonwealth of Virginia

Inadequate Security Measures Leading to Data Breach

Failing to secure personal data, leading to a breach where unauthorized individuals access the data. This is a violation under laws like GDPR and CCPA.

Source: General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), European Commission

Processing Sensitive Data Without Proper Safeguards

Handling sensitive information such as health data, racial or ethnic data, or children's data without obtaining explicit consent or implementing necessary safeguards, as required under GDPR and HIPAA.

Source: Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), U.S. Department of Health and Human Services

Failing to Provide Access to Data

Not responding to a consumer’s request to access their personal data within the legally mandated timeframe, which is a violation under GDPR and CCPA.

Source: GDPR Articles 12 and 15, European Commission

Processing Data Beyond Its Stated Purpose

Using data collected for one purpose (like processing a transaction) for another purpose (like targeted advertising) without obtaining new consent. This is prohibited under GDPR.

Source: GDPR Article 6(4), European Commission

Ignoring Data Deletion Requests (Right to be Forgotten)

Not deleting consumer data when requested, violating the "Right to be Forgotten" under GDPR and CCPA.

Source: GDPR Article 17, European Commission

Data Retention Beyond Legal Limits

Retaining personal data longer than necessary or beyond the period defined by law, violating GDPR or similar regulations.

Source: GDPR Article 5(1)(e), European Commission

Non-compliance with Children’s Data Protections

Collecting data from children under 13 without obtaining parental consent, as required by COPPA in the U.S.

Source: Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA), Federal Trade Commission

Not Conducting Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)

Failing to perform a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) when processing activities pose high risks to individuals, which is required under GDPR.

Source: GDPR Article 35, European Commission

Improper Data Transfer Practices

Transferring personal data across borders without ensuring adequate protection, as required under GDPR’s Chapter 5.

Source: GDPR Chapter 5, European Commission

Inaccurate or Incomplete Privacy Notices

Providing privacy notices that do not clearly or accurately inform users of data collection practices, which is a violation under GDPR and CCPA.

Source: CCPA, California Office of the Attorney General

Failure to Implement Data Minimization

Collecting more data than necessary for the intended purpose, violating the principle of data minimization under GDPR and CCPA.

Source: GDPR Article 5(1)(c), European Commission

GDPR Violations and Fines

Tracking Data Without Consent

Under GDPR, tracking users without their explicit consent—such as through cookies, analytics pixels, or other tracking mechanisms—is a direct violation. The GDPR mandates that consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous.

Source: EUR-Lex, Official Text of GDPR Regulation 2016/679

Not Honoring Data Subject Requests

GDPR grants several rights to data subjects, including the right to access their data, request corrections, and request deletion (the "right to be forgotten"). Failure to comply with these requests within one month constitutes a violation.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Articles 15-18

Data Breaches Due to Inadequate Security Measures

GDPR requires organizations to implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk. A data breach due to insufficient security measures is a violation.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Article 32

Improper Data Processing Beyond Original Purpose

Data must be processed for the specific purpose initially communicated to the data subject. Processing the data for any new purpose without additional consent is a violation.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Article 6(4)

Non-compliance with Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)

Organizations must conduct DPIAs when processing activities pose a high risk to individuals' rights and freedoms. Failing to do so can lead to significant fines under GDPR.

Source: EUR-Lex, GDPR Article 35

GDPR Fines

GDPR fines are categorized in two tiers:

  • Lower Tier: Up to €10 million, or 2% of the worldwide annual revenue of the prior financial year, whichever is higher.
  • Higher Tier: Up to €20 million, or 4% of the worldwide annual revenue of the prior financial year, whichever is higher.

Source: European Commission, GDPR Overview and Text
